JASHN E TIRGAN

Tirgan is a mid summer ancient Iranian festival, celebrated annually on Tir 13 (July 2, 3, or 4). It is celebrated by splashing water, dancing, reciting poetry, and serving traditional foods such as spinach soup and sholezard. The custom of tying rainbow-colored bands on wrists, which are worn for ten days and then thrown into a stream, is also a way to rejoice for children.

History about Jashn e Tirgan

Over time, Jashn-e Tirgan evolved into a cultural celebration that also commemorates an ancient Persian hero, Arash Kamangir, who sacrificed himself by shooting an arrow to determine the borders between Iran and Turan. This act led to the establishment of peace between the two kingdoms. Modern Tirgan festivities include music, dance, poetry recitals, and traditional rituals. One of the most notable customs involves tying colorful ribbons to trees near rivers or streams, symbolizing the intertwining of nature and human life. Water plays a central role, with people splashing water on each other as a symbol of purification and renewal.

More about Jashn e Tirgan

Jashn-e Tirgan is an ancient quarter year summer festival, celebrated about three months after the spring NoRuz. Tirgan is devoted to the divinity Tir and is associated with the dog-star Sirius and the coming of the rains in Iran and the fertility they bring.On this day it is customary to visit the Fire Temple to give thanks to Ahura Mazda, to participate in a jashan or thanksgiving ceremony, listen to stories of how the boundaries of Iran were established in antiquity with its Central Asian neighbour Turan (now Turkmenistan) by an archer shooting an arrow, share a community meal, play with rainbow bracelets made of seven coloured silks, splash each other with water, and dance and make merry.
